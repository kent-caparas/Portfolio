I have replayed this particular game enough times that I trust my own gut over
the wiki. And my gut said the crit rate was wrong. Not *unlucky* — wrong. So I
spent a weekend with a memory viewer and a notebook trying to prove it.

## the setup

The game runs fine in an emulator with a debugger attached, which means I could
freeze the frame, dump RAM, and watch values change between turns. The first
job was finding where damage gets computed. I parked a write-breakpoint on the
enemy HP address and let it trip.

```text
HP addr: 0x02_1A4C
write from: 0x08_3F12  (subtract)
operand source: 0x7E_00A8  (computed damage)
```

That gave me the damage value. Walking backwards from there, I found the
formula assembling itself out of a base power, a stat ratio, and — crucially —
a multiplier that was either `1.0` or `2.0`.

## the crit "chance"

Here's where it got interesting. The crit multiplier wasn't gated by a roll
against a crit-rate stat. It was gated by a check against the *low byte of a
global frame counter*:

```text
if (frame_counter & 0x0F) == 0  ->  crit
```

So crits weren't probabilistic at all. They fired on every sixteenth frame,
regardless of your stats. The "crit rate" item in the menu? Decorative. It
modified a value the damage routine never read.

This is why it *felt* off. My pattern-matching brain had correctly noticed that
crits clustered — they landed when my input timing happened to line up with the
counter — and then invented a superstition to explain it.

## the part where I was also wrong

I was *so* pleased with this discovery that I wrote it up, and then a friend who
actually knows the disassembly pointed out that I'd misread the mask. It's
`0x07`, not `0x0F` — crits fire every *eighth* frame, not every sixteenth. My
breakpoint had been catching a different code path for spell damage.

I'd done exactly what I accused the game of doing: found a pattern, locked onto
the first explanation, and stopped looking.

## what holds up

The core finding survives: crit in this game is deterministic, tied to a frame
counter, and the crit-rate stat is cosmetic. But the specific period was wrong
in my first draft, and the correction came from someone reading the code more
carefully than I did.

Reverse-engineering is just debugging where you don't have the source and the
author can't help you. The failure modes are identical. You will see what you
expect to see unless something forces you not to.
